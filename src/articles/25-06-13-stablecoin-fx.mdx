**Stablecoins are hot in Silicon Valley.** Since Stripe's $1.1B acquisition of Bridge,
the fintech world has regained interest in digital assets but with a sharp focus on their 
fiat currency-pegged constituents. 
These days, every fintech seems to be launching a "stablecoin account" offering some permutation of an 
embedded wallet, stablecoin payments, and yield. If you're like me and had 
only dealt with digital assets in the context of poorly informed investment 
decisions, this all seems rather puzzling – where is this stablecoin-linked 
economic activity coming from that warrants all of this investment and what 
benefits does it have over regular dollars? Even Jack Zhang (CEO of Airwallex) 
– someone whose fintech/business takes I think highly of – has been very public on his 
confusion with this fervor around stablecoins. 

## Where Stablecoins Have PMF

Building in the stablecoin FX space for the past few months, I've developed 
a lay of the land and takes on where I think stablecoins have or will find PMF. 

### Crypto Volatility Hedge

First off and most obvious, stablecoins have PMF as a volatility hedge for crypto investors. 
If you're investing in volatile assets, a highly liquid trading pair into a stable asset 
is exactly what you need to quickly get out of a position. Investors in 
fiat markets will use fiat currency for this but the crypto industry thrives 
in being a closed-loop financial system that doesn't rely on traditional banking. 
So stablecoins were developed and are used instead. 
Who wins here? The trading venues who make money on bid-ask spreads and the stablecoin issuers 
who make money off of yield of the pegged fiat currency held in reserve. 


### Currency Devaluation Hedge and Repatriation in Emerging Markets

In the exact same vein as a volatility hedge for crypto investors, many emerging markets 
struggle with volatile local currencies. Eventually, businesses and residents of these 
markets turn to more stable currencies to transact, often USD. Argentina 
is a classic example of this. However, foreign currency shortages are common and banking 
restrictions tend to be more conservative, leading locals to turn to
alternative means like crypto/stablecoins to transact with a stable currency outside 
of local banking. 

When I say there is PMF in these markets, I mean the demand is there. My co-founder and I 
have talked to hundreds of businesses across tens of industries with funds stuck in foreign 
markets that want to repatriate profits but can't due to currency shortages and central bank restrictions. 
Being in the grey area for most financial systems, stablecoins let you get around these costly 
and time-consuming restrictions but, by themselves, don't solve the liquidity problem. 
In countries like Malawi, foreign businesses want to repatriate funds but the liquidity 
just isn't there, fiat or otherwise. 

### Low-friction Global Payments

This is one of the most talked about use cases for stablecoins. International 
wires can be expensive, hard to automate due to poor open-banking support, and slow. 
In many cases, a counterparty's banks can restrict access to the funds altogether. 
Clearly, this whole process is rife with friction.

If you have a counterparty that is willing to accept stablecoins, you can get around 
all of this headache.
Where do you find such counterparties? In those same emerging markets where local 
currencies are in low demand and the banking system is weak. 

There are two use cases I've seen where these problems are most acute: 
1. Contractor payouts for gig-work like data-labelling. These are often 
in emerging markets where labour is cheaper but contractors have less access to 
financial service and banking options. 
2. Online purchaser businesses. These are businesses that get paid to wholesale 
purchase goods on their customer's behalf. Media buying and online travel agencies 
are prime examples of this. Since they are digital businesses, their customers 
are from all around the world. Businesses will often float funds while in-transit 
so are cashflow-sensitive and need fast global payment rails to get paid with.

## Where Stablecoins Will Likely Fail

### FX from Major Currencies

In the words of my co-founder, "liquidity is all you need". Until banks start 
trading stablecoins as they do in the fiat FX market, the bid-ask spread 
in stablecoin FX will be looser and will not be able to compete on price. 

Additionally, speed is FAR less of an issue among major currencies due to relying 
less on chains of correspondent banks to process transfers.  

## Benefits Summarized 

To summarize, stablecoins are creating real utility already and are likely to 
continue to do so. However, they are not in the places that Silicon Valley tech 
companies are used to serving: emerging markets, corporate finance departments, 
and cashflow-sensitive businesses. 

The value they generate is:
1. Regulatory arbitrage 
2. Modern banking, anywhere
